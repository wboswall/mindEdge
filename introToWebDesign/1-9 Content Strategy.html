													<me-users-mobile-app-prompt></me-users-mobile-app-prompt> <h3>Content Strategy</h3>

<p>The written content of any website is its most essential aspect. <a role="button" tabindex="0" class="glossary-alert" id="glossTerm0" data-term="content strategy" name="content strategy" title="The planning, creation, and management of content.">Content strategy<span class="screenreader">*</span></a><span style="display:none;">&nbsp;The planning, creation, and management of content.&nbsp;</span> is where all of the decision-making around that content begins.</p>

 <blockquote class="quotationblock">
<p>Content strategy helps organizations provide the right content, to the right people, at the right times, for the right reasons.</p>
<cite><i>Meghan Casey - The Content Strategy Toolkit</i></cite>
</blockquote>

<p>When done well, content strategy can:</p>

<div class="big yellow highlightbox">
<ul>
  <li>Assist companies in delivering the type of content their target audience desires.</li>
  <li>Provide a means for organizations to develop realistic, sustainable, and measurable publishing plans that keep their content on track in the long term.</li>
  <li>Cut costs and increase effectiveness by reducing redundant or extraneous content.</li>
  <li>Align communication across channels (web, print, social media) so that all outlets are working towards the same goals.</li>
  <li>Prevent web projects from being delayed due to underestimating the time needed to produce great content.</li>
</ul>
<cite>List adapted from <i> The Elements of Content Strategy by Erin Kissane.</i></cite>
</div>

<h4>Building a Content Strategy</h4>

<h5>Step 1: Content Inventory</h5>

<p>Building a content strategy begins with an inventory of the content you have&mdash; assuming you're working on an existing site. If you are creating a brand new site, content creation would obviously precede this step. The content inventory typically takes the form of a spreadsheet that lists every webpage, multimedia file, and PDF on the website. For each piece of content, the inventory spreadsheet usually shows:</p>

<div class="indentit">
<ul>
  <li>Title</li>
  <li>Format (standard text, video, PDF, etc.)</li>
  <li>URL or other location</li>
  <li>Content type (landing page, article, support page, contact page, etc.)</li>
  <li>Owner (person responsible for upkeep)</li>
</ul>
</div>

<p>An example of a content inventory spreadsheet is illustrated below.</p>

<img src="https://cdn-d.mindedgeonline.com/1671/content_screenshot.jpg" alt="screenshot of a spreadsheet containing a content inventory for a website." class="img-center" style="max-width:45em;">


<h5>Step 2: Content Audit</h5>

<p>Once you have this inventory, the next step is the audit. The purpose of the audit is to assess the relative strengths and weaknesses of all the content in order to prioritize future marketing activities.</p>

<p>In order to make those assessments, you have to be able to answer the following questions about your content in the context of whether it supports your key marketing/business objectives and fulfills your site users' needs.</p>

<p>Is the content:</p>

<div class="indentit">
<ul>
  <li>Appropriate?</li>
  <li>Useful and user-centered?</li>
  <li>Clear, consistent, and concise?</li>
  <li>Has it been properly supported, or is it outdated or inaccurate?</li>
</ul>
</div>

<p>Once you've inventoried and audited your content, the real work of content strategy begins, as you can see in Figure 1 in this document.</p>
